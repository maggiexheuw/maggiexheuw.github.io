<div class="section-label" id="motivation">§ 1 · Motivation</div>
<h2>Local Curvature, Global Topology</h2>

<div class="card">
<p>At first glance, <strong>curvature</strong> and <strong>topology</strong> belong to entirely different worlds. Curvature is a differential-geometric quantity: it depends on distances, angles, and derivatives — it can vary from point to point, and you can change it by deforming a surface. Topology, by contrast, is blind to such local data: a mug and a doughnut are topologically identical, no matter how you stretch or squash them.</p>

<p>The Gauss–Bonnet theorem reveals a stunning bridge between these two worlds. No matter how you deform a closed orientable surface, the total integral of its Gaussian curvature — a sum that feels completely analytic — always equals \( 2\pi \) times the Euler characteristic of that surface, an integer that does not change at all under continuous deformations.</p>

<p>In other words: <strong>add up all the local bending of a surface, and you recover a global topological fingerprint.</strong> Deform the surface, watch the curvature redistribute across every point — yet the integral refuses to budge.</p>
</div>

<div class="insight">
  <span class="insight-icon">💡</span>
  <p><strong>Core idea.</strong> The map \( M \mapsto \int_M K\,dA \) is a topological invariant: it assigns the same number to any two surfaces that can be continuously deformed into each other. Local differential geometry conspires, through cancellations, to produce a purely global count.</p>
</div>

<p>This document builds the theorem from the ground up: we introduce every definition needed, state and sketch the proof, work through illuminating examples, and then follow the historical thread that leads to Chern's far-reaching generalisation to manifolds of any even dimension.</p>

<div class="section-label" id="definitions">§ 2 · Fundamental Definitions</div>
<h2>The Building Blocks</h2>

<p>Let \( M \) be a smooth surface embedded in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \), locally parameterised by \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)\) where \((u,v)\) ranges over an open subset of \(\mathbb{R}^2\).</p>

<!-- 2.1 Tangent vectors & plane -->
<h3>2.1 Tangent Vectors and the Tangent Plane</h3>
<div class="card">
<p>At a point \( p = \mathbf{r}(u_0, v_0) \), the <strong>partial derivatives</strong></p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \mathbf{r}_u = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u}, \qquad \mathbf{r}_v = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v} \]
</div>
<p>are tangent vectors to the coordinate curves. Together they span the <strong>tangent plane</strong> \( T_p M \subset \mathbb{R}^3 \): the best flat approximation to \(M\) at \(p\). A vector \( \mathbf{v} \in T_p M \) can be written \( \mathbf{v} = a\,\mathbf{r}_u + b\,\mathbf{r}_v \) for some scalars \(a,b\).</p>
<p>The <strong>unit normal</strong> to the surface is</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v}{\|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v\|}. \]
</div>
</div>

<!-- 2.2 First fundamental form -->
<h3>2.2 The First Fundamental Form (Metric)</h3>
<div class="card">
<p>The <strong>first fundamental form</strong> \( g \) is the restriction of the Euclidean inner product to the tangent plane. For tangent vectors \( \mathbf{u} = a_1\mathbf{r}_u + b_1\mathbf{r}_v \) and \( \mathbf{w} = a_2\mathbf{r}_u + b_2\mathbf{r}_v \), it is given by</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ g(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{w}, \]
</div>
<p>with components (in the coordinate basis \(\{\mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v\}\))</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ E = \mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_u, \quad F = \mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_v, \quad G = \mathbf{r}_v\cdot\mathbf{r}_v, \]
</div>
<p>so the metric tensor is \( g = \begin{pmatrix} E & F \\ F & G \end{pmatrix} \). The first fundamental form encodes all <em>intrinsic</em> geometry: lengths of curves, areas of regions, and angles between tangent vectors.</p>

<p>The area element is \( dA = \sqrt{EG - F^2}\;du\,dv = \|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v\|\,du\,dv \).</p>
</div>

<!-- 2.3 Second fundamental form -->
<h3>2.3 The Second Fundamental Form</h3>
<div class="card">
<p>While the first fundamental form measures lengths within the surface, the <strong>second fundamental form</strong> \( h \) measures how the surface curves in the ambient space \(\mathbb{R}^3\). Its components are</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ e = \mathbf{r}_{uu}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \quad f = \mathbf{r}_{uv}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \quad g_2 = \mathbf{r}_{vv}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}, \]
</div>
<p>giving the matrix \( h = \begin{pmatrix} e & f \\ f & g_2 \end{pmatrix} \). For a tangent vector \(\mathbf{v}\), the quantity \( h(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{v}) \) is (up to sign convention) the <em>normal curvature</em> of the surface in the direction of \(\mathbf{v}\): how fast the surface bends away from its tangent plane in that direction.</p>
</div>

<!-- 2.4 Gauss map -->
<h3>2.4 The Gauss Map</h3>
<div class="card">
<p>The <strong>Gauss map</strong> is the smooth function</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \nu : M \longrightarrow S^2, \qquad p \longmapsto \hat{\mathbf{n}}(p), \]
</div>
<p>which sends each point of the surface to its outward unit normal, viewed as a point on the unit sphere \(S^2\). Its derivative \(d\nu_p : T_p M \to T_{\nu(p)}S^2\) measures how fast the normal direction rotates as you move across the surface. The <strong>shape operator</strong> (or Weingarten map) \(S_p = -d\nu_p\) is a self-adjoint linear map on \(T_pM\).</p>
<p>Intuitively: if the surface is very curved at \(p\), the normal rotates rapidly, and \(d\nu\) maps a small patch to a large patch on \(S^2\). If the surface is flat, the normal hardly moves at all.</p>
</div>

<!-- 2.5 Gaussian curvature -->
<h3>2.5 Gaussian Curvature \(K\)</h3>
<div class="card">
<p>The <strong>principal curvatures</strong> \(\kappa_1, \kappa_2\) at \(p\) are the eigenvalues of the shape operator \(S_p\). They give the maximum and minimum normal curvatures over all tangent directions.</p>
<p>The <strong>Gaussian curvature</strong> is their product:</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ K(p) = \kappa_1 \cdot \kappa_2 = \det(S_p) = \det(h) / \det(g). \]
</div>
<p>Equivalently, \(K\) is the ratio of the signed area stretched by the Gauss map: if \(U\) is a small patch around \(p\),</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ K(p) = \lim_{\text{diam}(U)\to 0} \frac{\text{area}(\nu(U))}{\text{area}(U)}. \]
</div>
<p>Gauss's <em>Theorema Egregium</em> ("Remarkable Theorem", 1827) states that \(K\) depends only on the first fundamental form — it is an <em>intrinsic</em> property. You can compute \(K\) using only measurements within the surface, without reference to the ambient \(\mathbb{R}^3\)!</p>
</div>

<div class="curv-row">
  <div class="curv-cell pos">
    <div class="label">Elliptic \( K > 0 \)</div>
    <p>Surface curves the same way in all directions. Example: sphere, ellipsoid. A small geodesic circle has <em>less</em> circumference than \(2\pi r\).</p>
  </div>
  <div class="curv-cell zero">
    <div class="label">Parabolic \( K = 0 \)</div>
    <p>Flat in one principal direction. Example: cylinder, cone. Can be unrolled onto a plane without stretching.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="curv-cell neg">
    <div class="label">Hyperbolic \( K &lt; 0 \)</div>
    <p>Saddle-shaped: curves up in one direction, down in another. Example: pseudosphere. Geodesic circles have <em>more</em> circumference than \(2\pi r\).</p>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- 2.6 Geodesic curvature -->
<h3>2.6 Geodesic Curvature \(k_g\)</h3>
<div class="card">
<p>For a smooth curve \(\gamma(s)\) parameterised by arc length on \(M\), the <strong>geodesic curvature</strong> measures how much the curve bends <em>within the surface</em> (as opposed to bending caused by the ambient curvature of the surface itself):</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ k_g = \ddot{\gamma} \cdot (\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times \dot{\gamma}), \]
</div>
<p>where \(\dot{\gamma}\) is the unit tangent and \(\ddot{\gamma} = \nabla_{\dot\gamma}\dot\gamma\) is the covariant acceleration.</p>
<ul>
  <li>A <strong>geodesic</strong> satisfies \(k_g \equiv 0\): it is the surface's notion of a "straight line." On a sphere, geodesics are great circles.</li>
  <li>The boundary \(\partial D\) of a planar disk traversed counterclockwise has \(k_g = 1/r\) (inverse radius), so \(\int k_g\,ds = 2\pi\).</li>
  <li>The geodesic curvature appears naturally in the <em>local</em> form of the Gauss–Bonnet theorem for regions with boundary.</li>
</ul>
</div>

<!-- 2.7 Euler characteristic -->
<h3>2.7 The Euler Characteristic \(\chi(M)\)</h3>
<div class="card">
<p>Given a triangulation of a closed orientable surface \(M\) — a decomposition into triangular faces — let \(V\), \(E\), \(F\) denote the number of vertices, edges, and faces respectively. The <strong>Euler characteristic</strong> is</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \chi(M) = V - E + F. \]
</div>
<p>Remarkably, this integer is <em>independent of the triangulation</em> chosen. It is a topological invariant of \(M\). For an orientable surface of genus \(g\) (i.e., with \(g\) handles),</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \chi(M_g) = 2 - 2g. \]
</div>
<table class="surfaces-table">
  <thead>
    <tr><th>Surface</th><th>Genus \(g\)</th><th>\(\chi = 2-2g\)</th><th>Example triangulation</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Sphere \(S^2\)</td><td class="math-cell">0</td><td class="math-cell">\(+2\)</td><td>Tetrahedron: \(V{=}4,\,E{=}6,\,F{=}4\)</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Torus \(T^2\)</td><td class="math-cell">1</td><td class="math-cell">\(0\)</td><td>\(V{=}1,\,E{=}3,\,F{=}2\) (minimal)</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Double torus</td><td class="math-cell">2</td><td class="math-cell">\(-2\)</td><td>—</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Genus-\(g\) surface</td><td class="math-cell">\(g\)</td><td class="math-cell">\(2-2g\)</td><td>—</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
</div>

<!-- ══════════════════════════════════════════════════ §3 THE THEOREM -->
<hr class="rule" />
<div class="section-label" id="theorem">§ 3 · The Theorem</div>
<h2>The Gauss–Bonnet Theorem</h2>

<div class="theorem">
  <div class="theorem-label">Theorem (Gauss–Bonnet, closed surface form)</div>
  <p>Let \(M\) be a compact, smooth, orientable surface without boundary embedded in \(\mathbb{R}^3\). Then</p>
  <div class="formula-box" style="background:#1e3a5f;">
    \[ \int_M K\,dA = 2\pi\,\chi(M). \]
  </div>
</div>

<h4>Dictionary of Symbols</h4>
<div class="def-grid">
  <div class="def-card">
    <div class="def-card-title">\(M\)</div>
    <p>The surface itself: a compact, smooth, orientable 2-dimensional manifold (without boundary, e.g. a sphere or torus).</p>
  </div>
  <div class="def-card">
    <div class="def-card-title">\(K\)</div>
    <p>The Gaussian curvature at each point of \(M\). It is a smooth function \(K: M \to \mathbb{R}\), varying from point to point.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="def-card">
    <div class="def-card-title">\(dA\)</div>
    <p>The area element (surface area measure). In local coordinates: \(dA = \sqrt{EG - F^2}\;du\,dv\). The integral is taken over all of \(M\).</p>
  </div>
  <div class="def-card">
    <div class="def-card-title">\(\chi(M)\)</div>
    <p>The Euler characteristic of \(M\): an integer topological invariant. For a genus-\(g\) surface, \(\chi = 2 - 2g\). It does not change under smooth deformations.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="def-card">
    <div class="def-card-title">\(2\pi\)</div>
    <p>The total signed angle of a full rotation in the plane — the normalisation that makes the formula exact over the integers.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="insight">
  <span class="insight-icon">🔍</span>
  <p><strong>What makes this surprising.</strong> The left side is analytic: it involves derivatives of the embedding. It changes if you deform the surface. The right side is an integer (times \(2\pi\)): it is completely rigid under continuous deformations. The theorem says these two quantities — one flexible, one frozen — are always equal.</p>
</div>

<!-- ══════════════════════════════════════════════════ §4 PROOF -->
<hr class="rule" />
<div class="section-label" id="proof">§ 4 · Proof Ideas</div>
<h2>How the Proof Works</h2>

<h3>4.1 The Local Gauss–Bonnet Formula</h3>
<div class="card">
<p>Let \(D \subset M\) be a compact region whose boundary \(\partial D\) is a piecewise smooth, closed curve with exterior angles \(\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k\) at the corners. The <strong>local Gauss–Bonnet formula</strong> states:</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \int_D K\,dA + \oint_{\partial D} k_g\,ds + \sum_{i=1}^k \theta_i = 2\pi\,\chi(D). \]
</div>
<p>This is Bonnet's generalisation of Gauss's geodesic triangle formula. Each term has a clear geometric meaning:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>\(\int_D K\,dA\):</strong> total Gaussian curvature of the interior.</li>
  <li><strong>\(\oint_{\partial D} k_g\,ds\):</strong> total geodesic curvature of the boundary (how much the boundary "bends" within the surface).</li>
  <li><strong>\(\sum \theta_i\):</strong> sum of the exterior angles at corner points of the boundary.</li>
</ul>
<p>For a smooth region without corners, \(\sum \theta_i = 0\). For a convex polygon with all boundary curves being geodesics (\(k_g = 0\)), this reduces to a pure angle-excess formula.</p>
</div>

<h3>4.2 Summing Over a Triangulation</h3>

<div class="proof-step">
  <div class="step-num">1</div>
  <div class="step-body">
    <p><strong>Triangulate \(M\).</strong> Cover \(M\) by a finite collection of geodesic triangles \(\{\Delta_j\}\). Each triangle \(\Delta_j\) has three geodesic sides (\(k_g = 0\) on each side) and three interior angles \(\alpha_j, \beta_j, \gamma_j\). The exterior angle at each vertex of a triangle is \(\pi\) minus the interior angle.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="proof-step">
  <div class="step-num">2</div>
  <div class="step-body">
    <p><strong>Apply local Gauss–Bonnet to each triangle.</strong> For a geodesic triangle \(\Delta_j\), the boundary curves are geodesics (\(k_g = 0\)) and the three exterior angles are \(\pi - \alpha_j\), \(\pi - \beta_j\), \(\pi - \gamma_j\). The local formula gives:</p>
    <div class="formula-box" style="background:#1e3a5f; font-size:.98rem;">
      \[ \int_{\Delta_j} K\,dA + 0 + (\pi - \alpha_j) + (\pi - \beta_j) + (\pi - \gamma_j) = 2\pi \]
    \[ \Longrightarrow \quad \int_{\Delta_j} K\,dA = (\alpha_j + \beta_j + \gamma_j) - \pi. \]
    </div>
    <p>This is the classical <em>spherical excess</em> formula: the integral of curvature over a geodesic triangle equals its angle sum minus \(\pi\).</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="proof-step">
  <div class="step-num">3</div>
  <div class="step-body">
    <p><strong>Sum over all triangles.</strong> Adding over all \(F\) triangles:</p>
    <div class="formula-box" style="background:#1e3a5f; font-size:.98rem;">
      \[ \int_M K\,dA = \sum_j (\alpha_j + \beta_j + \gamma_j) - \pi F. \]
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="proof-step">
  <div class="step-num">4</div>
  <div class="step-body">
    <p><strong>Count angle sums at vertices.</strong> At each interior vertex \(v\), the angles of all triangles meeting at \(v\) sum to exactly \(2\pi\) (since the surface is smooth there). Thus \(\sum_j (\alpha_j + \beta_j + \gamma_j) = 2\pi V\).</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="proof-step">
  <div class="step-num">5</div>
  <div class="step-body">
    <p><strong>Count edges.</strong> Each edge of the triangulation is shared by exactly 2 triangles. A triangulation of a closed surface satisfies \(3F = 2E\) (three edges per triangle, each shared twice), so \(E = \tfrac{3}{2}F\). Substituting:</p>
    <div class="formula-box" style="background:#1e3a5f; font-size:.98rem;">
      \[ \int_M K\,dA = 2\pi V - \pi F = 2\pi V - 2\pi E + 2\pi F - \pi F - 2\pi E + 2\pi E \]
    \[ = 2\pi(V - E + F) = 2\pi\,\chi(M). \]
    </div>
    <p>More cleanly: \(2\pi V - \pi F = 2\pi V - \pi\cdot\tfrac{2}{3}\cdot 3F/2\)... the direct route is: \(\pi F = 2\pi E - 2\pi V\) gives \(2\pi V - \pi F = 2\pi(V-E+F) = 2\pi\chi(M)\), using \(3F = 2E \Rightarrow E = 3F/2\) and \(\pi F = \pi F\), \(2\pi E = 3\pi F\): so \(2\pi V - \pi F = 2\pi V - 3\pi F + 2\pi F = 2\pi(V-E+F)\).\(\square\)</p>
  </div>
</div>

<h3>4.3 The Geodesic Triangle on a Sphere</h3>
<div class="example">
  <div class="example-label">Worked Computation</div>
  <p>Take the unit sphere \(S^2\) (\(K \equiv 1\)) and a geodesic triangle \(\Delta\) with interior angles \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\). Since \(K=1\), the area integral equals the area:</p>
  \[ \int_\Delta K\,dA = \text{Area}(\Delta). \]
  <p>The local Gauss–Bonnet formula (for geodesic boundary, so \(k_g = 0\), and exterior angles \(\pi-\alpha, \pi-\beta, \pi-\gamma\)) gives:</p>
  \[ \text{Area}(\Delta) + 0 + (\pi-\alpha)+(\pi-\beta)+(\pi-\gamma) = 2\pi \]
  \[ \Longrightarrow \quad \text{Area}(\Delta) = \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi. \]
  <p>This is the classical <strong>spherical excess formula</strong>: the area of a spherical triangle equals the excess of its angle sum over \(\pi\). A triangle with angles \(\alpha=\beta=\gamma=90°=\pi/2\) has area \(3\pi/2 - \pi = \pi/2\), which indeed is one-eighth of \(4\pi\). ✓</p>
</div>

<!-- ══════════════════════════════════════════════════ §5 EXAMPLES -->
<hr class="rule" />
<div class="section-label" id="examples">§ 5 · Examples</div>
<h2>Gauss–Bonnet in Action</h2>

<!-- Example 1: Sphere -->
<div class="example">
  <div class="example-label">Example 1 — Sphere of Radius \(R\)</div>
  <p>The sphere \(S^2_R\) of radius \(R\) has constant Gaussian curvature \(K = 1/R^2\) and total area \(4\pi R^2\). Therefore:</p>
  \[ \int_{S^2_R} K\,dA = \frac{1}{R^2} \cdot 4\pi R^2 = 4\pi = 2\pi \cdot 2, \]
  <p>which gives \(\chi(S^2) = 2\). ✓ (The sphere has genus 0.)</p>
  <p>Notice: if you inflate the sphere (increase \(R\)), the curvature \(K=1/R^2\) decreases, but the area \(4\pi R^2\) increases proportionally, so the integral stays at \(4\pi\). This is Gauss–Bonnet at work: the integral is rigid even as the surface deforms.</p>
</div>

<!-- Example 2: Torus -->
<div class="example">
  <div class="example-label">Example 2 — Torus</div>
  <p>Embed the standard torus in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) with major radius \(R\) and minor radius \(r\) (with \(R > r\)). In angular coordinates \((\phi, \theta)\), the Gaussian curvature is:</p>
  \[ K(\phi, \theta) = \frac{\cos\phi}{r(R + r\cos\phi)}. \]
  <p>The area element is \(dA = r(R + r\cos\phi)\,d\phi\,d\theta\). So the integrand is:</p>
  \[ K\,dA = \frac{\cos\phi}{r(R+r\cos\phi)} \cdot r(R+r\cos\phi)\,d\phi\,d\theta = \cos\phi\,d\phi\,d\theta. \]
  <p>Integrating over \(\phi,\theta \in [0,2\pi]\):</p>
  \[ \int_T K\,dA = \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta \int_0^{2\pi} \cos\phi\,d\phi = 2\pi \cdot [\sin\phi]_0^{2\pi} = 2\pi \cdot 0 = 0 = 2\pi \cdot 0. \]
  <p>So \(\chi(T^2) = 0\). ✓ Geometrically: the outer half of the torus (where \(\cos\phi > 0\)) has <em>positive</em> curvature (like a sphere), while the inner half has <em>negative</em> curvature (saddle-shaped). They cancel exactly.</p>
</div>

<!-- Example 3: Disk with boundary -->
<div class="example">
  <div class="example-label">Example 3 — Flat Disk (with Boundary)</div>
  <p>Consider the flat unit disk \(D \subset \mathbb{R}^2\). Here \(K \equiv 0\) (flat plane), the boundary \(\partial D\) is a circle of radius 1 traversed counterclockwise with geodesic curvature \(k_g = 1\), and \(\chi(D) = 1\) (disk is contractible).</p>
  <p>The local Gauss–Bonnet formula (no corners, so \(\sum\theta_i = 0\)):</p>
  \[ \underbrace{\int_D K\,dA}_{0} + \underbrace{\oint_{\partial D} k_g\,ds}_{1\cdot 2\pi} + 0 = 2\pi \cdot 1 \]
  \[ 0 + 2\pi = 2\pi. \;\checkmark \]
  <p>This reflects the intuition that a circle "turns" by exactly \(2\pi\) as you traverse it, regardless of the shape of its interior.</p>
</div>

<!-- Example 4: Geodesic Triangle on Unit Sphere -->
<div class="example">
  <div class="example-label">Example 4 — Right-Angle Triangle on the Unit Sphere</div>
  <p>On the unit sphere \(S^2\) (\(K \equiv 1\)), take the triangle with vertices at the north pole \(N\), and two equatorial points separated by a quarter-circle. The three angles are all \(\alpha = \beta = \gamma = \pi/2\). By the spherical excess formula:</p>
  \[ \int_\Delta K\,dA = \text{Area}(\Delta) = \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi = \frac{\pi}{2}+\frac{\pi}{2}+\frac{\pi}{2}-\pi = \frac{\pi}{2}. \]
  <p>One can verify directly: this triangle is exactly \(\frac{1}{8}\) of the full sphere (by symmetry), so Area\((\Delta) = \frac{1}{8}\cdot 4\pi = \frac{\pi}{2}\). ✓</p>
</div>

<!-- ══════════════════════════════════════════════════ §6 HISTORY -->
<hr class="rule" />
<div class="section-label" id="history">§ 6 · Historical Development</div>
<h2>From Gauss to Chern</h2>

<p>The Gauss–Bonnet theorem as we know it emerged gradually over more than a century, with each generation extending the domain of validity and deepening the conceptual understanding.</p>

<div class="timeline">

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-dot">G</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <div class="tl-year">1827</div>
      <div class="tl-title">Gauss and the Theorema Egregium</div>
      <p>Carl Friedrich Gauss proved that the Gaussian curvature \(K\) is an intrinsic quantity — determined entirely by the metric of the surface, independent of how it is embedded in space. He also established the angle-excess formula for geodesic triangles on a sphere: \(\text{Area}(\Delta) = \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi\), with the curvature implicitly present since \(K = 1\) on the unit sphere.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-dot">B</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <div class="tl-year">1848</div>
      <div class="tl-title">Bonnet: Boundary Terms and General Regions</div>
      <p>Pierre Ossian Bonnet formulated the full local version of the theorem: for a region \(D\) with piecewise smooth boundary on an arbitrary surface, \(\int_D K\,dA + \oint_{\partial D} k_g\,ds + \sum\theta_i = 2\pi\chi(D)\). This was the first version to handle boundary curvature systematically. The global theorem for closed surfaces follows as a special case (no boundary, so all boundary terms vanish).</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-dot">H</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <div class="tl-year">1926</div>
      <div class="tl-title">Hopf: Even-Dimensional Hypersurfaces</div>
      <p>Heinz Hopf extended the theorem to closed hypersurfaces (codimension-1 submanifolds) in odd-dimensional Euclidean spaces, proving that the degree of the Gauss map equals \(\tfrac{1}{2}\chi(M)\). He conjectured a higher-dimensional intrinsic version for general Riemannian manifolds.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-dot">A</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <div class="tl-year">1940</div>
      <div class="tl-title">Allendoerfer, Fenchel, Weil: Extrinsic Proofs</div>
      <p>Carl Allendoerfer and Werner Fenchel independently proved, in 1940, a Gauss–Bonnet theorem for Riemannian manifolds embedded in Euclidean space of any dimension, using the "tube method" (Weyl's tube formula). André Weil simultaneously gave an equivalent extrinsic proof. These proofs still required an ambient space.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-dot">C</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <div class="tl-year">1944</div>
      <div class="tl-title">Chern: The Intrinsic Proof and Transgression</div>
      <p>Shiing-Shen Chern gave the first fully intrinsic proof — valid for any compact Riemannian manifold of even dimension, without requiring any embedding. His key innovation was to work on the unit sphere bundle \(SM\) of \(M\), where he found a differential form \(\Pi\) (the <em>transgression</em> of the Euler form) such that \(d\Pi = e(\Omega)\), and then applied Stokes' theorem globally. This proof is the foundation of modern characteristic class theory.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tl-item">
    <div class="tl-dot">CW</div>
    <div class="tl-content">
      <div class="tl-year">1950s</div>
      <div class="tl-title">Chern–Weil Theory and Characteristic Classes</div>
      <p>Chern and Weil developed a systematic framework (Chern–Weil theory) for constructing global topological invariants from local curvature data. Given a principal \(G\)-bundle with connection, any invariant polynomial on the Lie algebra of \(G\) gives a closed differential form whose de Rham cohomology class is a topological invariant. The Gauss–Bonnet–Chern theorem is the special case where the bundle is the frame bundle, \(G = O(2m)\), and the polynomial is the Pfaffian.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

</div>

<!-- ══════════════════════════════════════════════════ §7 GBC THEOREM -->
<hr class="rule" />
<div class="section-label" id="generalisation">§ 7 · The High-Dimensional Generalisation</div>
<h2>The Gauss–Bonnet–Chern Theorem</h2>

<div class="card">
<p>Let \(M\) be a compact, oriented Riemannian manifold of dimension \(2m\) (note: only even dimensions occur, since \(\chi(M) = 0\) for odd-dimensional closed manifolds). Let \(\Omega\) denote the <strong>curvature 2-form</strong> of the Levi-Civita connection, viewed as a skew-symmetric matrix of 2-forms.</p>

<h4>The Pfaffian</h4>
<p>The key ingredient is the <strong>Pfaffian</strong> of the curvature matrix. For a \(2m \times 2m\) skew-symmetric matrix \(A\), the Pfaffian \(\text{Pf}(A)\) is the polynomial defined by</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \text{Pf}(A)^2 = \det(A), \]
</div>
<p>with a chosen sign convention. Explicitly,</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \text{Pf}(A) = \frac{1}{2^m m!} \sum_{\sigma \in S_{2m}} \text{sgn}(\sigma)\, A_{\sigma(1)\sigma(2)}\cdots A_{\sigma(2m-1)\sigma(2m)}. \]
</div>
<p>When \(m=1\) (surfaces), \(\text{Pf}\begin{pmatrix}0 & K\, dA \\ -K\,dA & 0\end{pmatrix} = K\,dA\), recovering the usual curvature form.</p>

<h4>The Gauss–Bonnet–Chern Formula</h4>
<div class="theorem">
  <div class="theorem-label">Theorem (Gauss–Bonnet–Chern)</div>
  <p>For a compact oriented Riemannian manifold \(M^{2m}\) without boundary:</p>
  <div class="formula-box" style="background:#1e3a5f;">
    \[ \int_M \operatorname{Pf}\!\left(\frac{\Omega}{2\pi}\right) = \chi(M). \]
  </div>
  <p>Here \(\Omega/(2\pi)\) means each curvature 2-form entry is divided by \(2\pi\), and \(\operatorname{Pf}(\Omega/(2\pi))\) is the resulting \(2m\)-form (a top-degree form on \(M\)), which can be integrated over \(M\).</p>
</div>

<h4>Recovering the 2D Case</h4>
<p>When \(m=1\), the manifold is a surface and \(\text{Pf}(\Omega/2\pi) = K\,dA/(2\pi)\), so:</p>
<div class="formula-box">
  \[ \int_M \frac{K\,dA}{2\pi} = \chi(M) \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \int_M K\,dA = 2\pi\,\chi(M). \]
</div>

<h4>Why the Pfaffian?</h4>
<p>The Pfaffian arises because the Euler class \(e \in H^{2m}(M;\mathbb{R})\) is a characteristic class of the tangent bundle. By Chern–Weil theory, every characteristic class can be represented by a closed differential form built from the curvature. The Euler class is precisely represented by \(\text{Pf}(\Omega/2\pi)\). The Gauss–Bonnet–Chern theorem says that integrating this representative gives the topological Euler characteristic \(\chi(M) = \langle e(TM), [M]\rangle\) — the pairing of the Euler class with the fundamental class.</p>

<h4>Low-Dimensional Instances</h4>
<ul>
  <li><strong>\(2m = 2\):</strong> The classical Gauss–Bonnet theorem, \(\int_M K\,dA = 2\pi\chi(M)\).</li>
  <li><strong>\(2m = 4\):</strong> For a 4-dimensional Riemannian manifold, the integrand involves a quadratic expression in the Riemann curvature components — the formula becomes \(\int_M \frac{1}{8\pi^2}(|R|^2 - 4|\text{Ric}|^2 + R_{\rm sc}^2/4)\,dV = \chi(M)\), where \(R\), \(\text{Ric}\), \(R_{\rm sc}\) denote the full Riemann tensor, Ricci tensor, and scalar curvature respectively.</li>
</ul>
</div>

<!-- ══════════════════════════════════════════════════ §8 CONCLUSION -->
<hr class="rule" />
<div class="section-label" id="conclusion">§ 8 · Conclusion</div>

<div class="conclusion">
  <h2>Geometry Speaks Topology</h2>
  <p>The Gauss–Bonnet theorem is one of the most beautiful results in all of mathematics. It reveals that the local, differential-geometric concept of curvature — how a surface bends in space at each individual point — carries global, topological information about the shape as a whole: information that cannot change no matter how you deform the surface.</p>
  <p>The formula</p>
  <div class="big-formula">\( \displaystyle \int_M K\,dA = 2\pi\,\chi(M) \)</div>
  <p>says: add up all the bending, everywhere, and you get a topological integer. Deform the surface — watch curvature flow from one region to another — and the total refuses to change. This is the geometry–topology miracle.</p>
  <p>Chern's generalisation</p>
  <div class="big-formula">\( \displaystyle \int_M \operatorname{Pf}\!\left(\tfrac{\Omega}{2\pi}\right) = \chi(M) \)</div>
  <p>extends this miracle to every even-dimensional compact Riemannian manifold, embedding it in the vast machinery of characteristic class theory. The idea that curvature, encoded in a principal bundle's connection, determines topological invariants is the seed of modern mathematical physics — it underlies gauge theories, index theorems, and much of contemporary geometry.</p>
  <p style="color:rgba(255,255,255,.55); font-size:.9rem; margin-top:28px;">The profound bridge between local differential geometry and global topology is not an accident — it is the central theme of modern mathematics.</p>
</div>
